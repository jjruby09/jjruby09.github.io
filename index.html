About

The Core Thread

Science is the iterative process of building models of how nature works, testing them against data, and updating your beliefs accordingly. That's Bayesian inference at its core — and it applies whether you're diagnosing a laser-driven fusion implosion or projecting a pitcher's future performance.

My career has been structured around one question: what can the data actually tell us? Not what we want it to say, not what confirms existing intuition — what the signal actually supports once you've honestly accounted for the noise.

I moved from experimental high-energy-density physics into professional baseball operations because the underlying methodology is identical. Both fields require constructing rigorous probabilistic models, running them against expensive experimental data, and making decisions under meaningful uncertainty with real consequences.

The tools are Bayesian statistics, Monte Carlo methods, and the discipline to distinguish between a genuine result and a story you told yourself.

Work

Background & Experience

High-Energy-Density Physics & Fusion Science

Laboratory for Laser Energetics · Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory

My scientific foundation was built studying inertial confinement fusion (ICF) and high-energy-density (HED) physics — regimes where intense laser pulses drive matter to extreme pressures and temperatures to test fundamental models of plasma behavior.

  • Designed and fielded experiments on the OMEGA laser facility studying laser-driven implosion dynamics, hydrodynamic instabilities, and nuclear burn physics.
  • Applied Bayesian inference and MCMC methods to diagnose implosion performance from nuclear and X-ray observables, separating physical signal from complex experimental noise floors.
  • Built simulation workflows coupling radiation-hydrodynamics codes to diagnostic forward models to enable principled comparison of theory to experiment.
  • Contributed to the understanding of energy coupling, mix, and stagnation physics critical to the ignition roadmap at the National Ignition Facility.

Baseball Research & Development

Houston Astros — Senior Director, R&D

Leading the R&D function for one of Major League Baseball's most analytically sophisticated organizations. The work is the same as physics: build honest models of complex systems, test them against observed outcomes, and inform decisions under uncertainty.

  • Direct research portfolio spanning player evaluation, biomechanics, in-game strategy, and player development — with a focus on translational research that actually influences decisions.
  • Build and deploy probabilistic models of player performance and development trajectories using hierarchical Bayesian frameworks and modern machine learning methods.
  • Bridge the gap between research and baseball operations — translating statistical findings into actionable, decision-relevant outputs for scouts, coaches, and front-office leadership.
  • Maintain a rigorous culture of epistemic honesty: models that acknowledge their own uncertainty are more useful than those that don't.

Operating Principles

Leadership Philosophy

These aren't aspirational statements — they're operating constraints. Violating them consistently produces bad science and bad organizations.

Servant Leadership

The job of leadership is to remove obstacles, provide clarity, and create conditions where talented people can do their best work. Authority is a tool for enabling others, not for self-amplification. Good decisions come from the person closest to the problem, not necessarily the most senior person in the room.

Integrity as a Practical Necessity

Intellectual honesty isn't a virtue signal — it's a prerequisite for doing useful work. In science and in sports analytics, the cost of believing a false model is real and measurable. Being honest about what the data says, including when it contradicts your prior or your preferred narrative, is the only sustainable path.

Psychological Safety & Failure

Discovery requires being wrong. If the environment punishes people for surfacing a bad result, reporting a failed experiment, or disagreeing with leadership, you've destroyed your own feedback loop. Building high psychological safety isn't soft management — it's sound epistemology. The team that can fail openly is the team that learns fastest.
